Why we exist

AI is reshaping who gets to work, learn, and be heard. We're making sure the people most likely to be left out get a seat at the table.

Every wave of technology quietly draws a line: who it's built for, and who gets to catch up later. We work on the wrong side of that line on purpose — teaching the tools, plainly and patiently, to the communities the rollout usually forgets.

About us

Art, learning, AI. Minus the hype.

We make things that help people get on with AI — shows, classes, the odd workshop. The goal is for it to feel useful and a bit beautiful, and not like a sales pitch.

What we actually do sits between AI-inspired art, education, and public-facing events — meant for communities, schools, organisations, and anyone who's curious but a little tired of the noise.

We're a handful of Singapore-based finance and tech people — "young seniors", if you like — who've put money into local startups and gotten some out. We started Sagelion partly so we'd stay close to where culture, technology, and markets are actually going, and partly because we want other Singaporeans (especially the ones feeling squeezed by AI) to navigate this stretch of the digital economy with taste, rigour, and a bit of heart.

Community work. Slow, useful, hopefully built to last.

Field notes 21 June 2026

The workshop ran. Here's what actually happened.

Yesterday, 9:30am, Emmaus Church. PMETs, educators, retirees — a proper mixed room. We had two and a half hours, a working projector (eventually), and a plan that held up better than expected.

We kept the structure lean: thirty minutes of concepts, forty minutes of real examples — report summarisation, meeting notes, data analysis — then a break, then people at actual stations doing it themselves. Three stations: ChatGPT on the web, Otter.ai for transcription, Google Sheets with AI. No logins required, QR codes for access, demo documents already loaded. The goal was to remove every possible reason to not start.

The bit that worked best was Part 5 — Julie asked me questions live over Telegram, I responded on screen with step-by-step answers, and she translated the key points for the room in real time. Watching people see an AI reason through something in plain language, in front of them, with someone they trusted doing the asking — that's when the room shifted. Less "interesting technology" and more "oh, this is actually useful."

Sean also spoke — a 24-hour OpenClaw user at the time, still finding his feet with it. No polish, no rehearsed pitch. Just someone who'd picked it up the day before and was willing to say what it felt like. That honesty landed harder than any demo we could have run. When the room heard from someone exactly where they were — not an expert, not a salesperson — something unlocked. Afterwards, participants told us: "their eyes were open." We'll take that.

We also screened this during the session.

An AI-generated music video — our own work, made with the same tools we teach. Showing people what's possible before they try it themselves.

@8ktogo

What we'll change next time: build in more setup buffer before 9:30. The gap between people who arrived laptop-open and those who needed five minutes just to connect to wifi was wider than we planned for. Not a problem — it's exactly who we're here for. But the agenda needs to absorb it rather than fight it. We're also going to formalise the DeepSeek vs Claude comparison — participants kept coming back to it.
